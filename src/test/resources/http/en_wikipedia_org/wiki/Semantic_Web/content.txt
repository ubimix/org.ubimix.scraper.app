<p>The <b>Semantic Web</b> is a "man-made woven web of data" that facilitates <a href="/wiki/Machine#Computing_machines" title="Machine">machines</a> to understand the <a href="/wiki/Semantics" title="Semantics">semantics</a>, or meaning, of information on the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a><sup id="cite_ref-w3c_faq_0-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-w3c_faq-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup>. The concept of Semantic Web applies methods beyond linear presentation of information (<a href="/wiki/Web_1.0" title="Web 1.0">Web 1.0</a>) and multi-linear presentation of information (<a href="/wiki/Web_2.0" title="Web 2.0">Web 2.0</a>) to make use of <i>hyper-structures</i> leading to entities of hypertext.</p>
<p>The concept of the <i>Semantic Network Model</i> was coined in the early sixties by the cognitive scientist <a href="/wiki/Allan_M._Collins" title="Allan M. Collins">Allan M. Collins</a>, linguist <a href="/w/index.php?title=M._Ross_Quillian&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="M. Ross Quillian (page does not exist)">M. Ross Quillian</a> and psychologist <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_F._Loftus" title="Elizabeth F. Loftus" class="mw-redirect">Elizabeth F. Loftus</a> in various publications<sup id="cite_ref-Collins1969_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Collins1969-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Collins1970_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Collins1970-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Collins1975_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Collins1975-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-M._Ross_Quillian_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-M._Ross_Quillian-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-M._Ross_Quillian_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-M._Ross_Quillian-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> as a form to represent semantically structured knowledge. It extends the network of <a href="/wiki/Hyperlink" title="Hyperlink">hyperlinked</a> human-readable <a href="/wiki/Web_pages" title="Web pages" class="mw-redirect">web pages</a> by inserting machine-readable <a href="/wiki/Metadata" title="Metadata">metadata</a> about pages and how they are related to each other, enabling automated agents to access the Web more intelligently and perform tasks on behalf of users. The term was coined by <a href="/wiki/Tim_Berners-Lee" title="Tim Berners-Lee">Tim Berners-Lee</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> the inventor of the World Wide Web and director of the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web_Consortium" title="World Wide Web Consortium">World Wide Web Consortium</a> ("<a href="/wiki/W3C" title="W3C" class="mw-redirect">W3C</a>"), which oversees the development of proposed Semantic Web standards. He defines the Semantic Web as "a web of data that can be processed directly and indirectly by machines."</p>
<p>Many of the technologies proposed by the W3C already existed before positioned under the W3C umbrella. These are used in various contexts, particularly those dealing with information that encompasses a limited and defined domain, and where sharing data is a common necessity, such as scientific research or data exchange among businesses. In addition, other technologies with similar goals have emerged, such as <a href="/wiki/Microformat" title="Microformat">microformats</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Semantic_Web&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Purpose and preparedness">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Purpose_and_preparedness">Purpose and preparedness</span></h2>
<p>Its critics have questioned its feasibility, however, more and more applications in research for and within industry, biology and human sciences research prove the validity of the original concept<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup>.</p>
<p>The Semantic Web as originally envisioned is a system that enables machines to understand and respond to complex human requests based on their meaning. It has remained in a permanent state of improvement of its framework of standards. It is understood today that <i>understanding</i> by machines within the available repositories of information requires prior systematic <i>structuring</i> of the contents and strategic proliferation of tools for performing such structuring in a layered approach. There is no escaping the fact, the availability of understandable and intelligible information, even in automated processing, needs time for preparation.</p>
<p>The reluctance of the most highly knowledgeable class to structure their personal understanding of the world where they live and work appears partially driven by shortages of time, preparedness and willingness to contribute. The supplicants for deep knowledge appear to stay hampered by lack of technological knowledge and lack of financing for the development of the data bases and the related work<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup>. The growing interest in structuring and access to this knowledge, however raises the questions of where to start and what to do first<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup>.</p>
<p>The main purpose of the <b>Semantic Web</b> is driving the evolution of the current Web by enabling users to find, share, and combine information more easily. Humans are capable of using the Web to carry out tasks such as finding the <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a> word for "folder", reserving a library book, and searching for the lowest price for a DVD. However, <a href="/wiki/Machine#Computing_machines" title="Machine">machines</a> cannot accomplish all of these tasks without human direction, because web pages are designed to be read by people, not machines. The semantic web is a vision of information that can be readily interpreted by machines, so machines can perform more of the tedious work involved in finding, combining, and acting upon information on the web.</p>
<p>Tim Berners-Lee originally expressed the vision of the Semantic Web as follows:<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="toccolours" style="float:none; padding: 10px 15px 10px 15px; display:table;">
<p>I have a dream for the Web [in which computers] become capable of analyzing all the data on the Web&#160;– the content, links, and transactions between people and computers. A ‘Semantic Web’, which should make this possible, has yet to emerge, but when it does, the day-to-day mechanisms of trade, bureaucracy and our daily lives will be handled by machines talking to machines. The ‘<a href="/wiki/Intelligent_agent" title="Intelligent agent">intelligent agents</a>’ people have touted for ages will finally materialize.</p>
<p style="text-align: right;">— <cite><a href="/wiki/Tim_Berners-Lee" title="Tim Berners-Lee">Tim Berners-Lee</a>, 1999</cite></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The Semantic Web is regarded as an integrator across different content, information applications and systems. It also provides mechanisms for the realization of <a href="/wiki/Enterprise_Information_System" title="Enterprise Information System">Enterprise Information Systems</a>. Rapid growth in the volume of data on the web provides the impetus for researchers to focus on the creation and dissemination of innovative Semantic Web technologies to facilitate automated processing. Often the terms ’<a href="/wiki/Semantics" title="Semantics">Semantics</a>’, ’<a href="/wiki/Metadata" title="Metadata">metadata</a>’, ’<a href="/wiki/Ontologies" title="Ontologies" class="mw-redirect">ontologies</a>’ and ’Semantic Web’ are used inconsistently. In particular, these terms are used as everyday terminology by researchers and practitioners, spanning a vast landscape of different fields, technologies, concepts and application areas. Furthermore, there is confusion with regard to the current status of the enabling technologies envisioned to realize the Semantic Web. In a paper presented by Gerber, Barnard and Van der Merwe<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> the Semantic Web landscape is charted and a brief summary of related terms and enabling technologies is presented. The architectural model proposed by Tim Berners-Lee is used as basis to present a status model that reflects current and emerging technologies.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Semantic_Web&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Semantic publishing">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Semantic_publishing">Semantic publishing</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Semantic_publishing" title="Semantic publishing">Semantic publishing</a> will greatly benefit from the Semantic Web. In particular, the Semantic Web is expected to revolutionize <a href="/wiki/Academic_publishing" title="Academic publishing">scientific publishing</a>, such as real-time publishing and sharing of experimental data on the Internet. This simple but radical idea is now<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style_(dates_and_numbers)#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style (dates and numbers)" class="mw-redirect"><span title="The time period in the vicinity of this tag is ambiguous from August 2011">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> being explored by <a href="/wiki/W3C" title="W3C" class="mw-redirect">W3C</a> HCLS group's <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://esw.w3.org/topic/HCLS/ScientificPublishingTaskForce">Scientific Publishing Task Force</a>.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from August 2011">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Semantic_Web&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Semantic blogging">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Semantic_blogging">Semantic blogging</span></h3>
<p>Semantic blogging, like semantic publishing, may change the way blogs are read. Currently "the process of blogging inherently emphasizes metadata creation more than traditional Web publishing methodologies".<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> Some blog users already tag their entries with topics, allowing for easier migration into a semantic web environment. It is intentionally saved in not only a human-readable format, but also in a machine-readable format as the tags can be linked easily to other blogs containing similar information. When a release of a game or movie occurs, bloggers tend to rate them using their own system. If there were to be a unified system, these blogs could automatically become assimilated using similar semantics and give a user a score when searching using a semantic search. RSS feeds are another way that blogs already have machine-readable data that is easily accessible by the semantic web.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Semantic_Web&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Web 3.0">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Web_3.0">Web 3.0</span></h3>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Web_2.0#Web_3.0" title="Web 2.0">Web 3.0</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tim_Berners-Lee" title="Tim Berners-Lee">Tim Berners-Lee</a> has described the semantic web as a component of 'Web 3.0'.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>"Semantic Web" is sometimes used as a synonym for "Web 3.0", though each term's definition may vary depending on whom you ask. Many believe that Web 3.0 is the "next big thing"<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from July 2011">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> but there only lies speculation as to just what that might be. It will be an improvement in the respect that it will still contain <a href="/wiki/Web_2.0" title="Web 2.0">Web 2.0</a> properties while continuing to add to its ever expanding lexicon and library of applications. There are some who claim that Web 3.0 will be more application based and center its efforts towards more graphically capable environments, "non-browser applications and non-computer based devices...geographic or location-based information retrieval" and even more applicable use and growth of Artificial Intelligence.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> For example, <a href="/wiki/Conrad_Wolfram" title="Conrad Wolfram">Conrad Wolfram</a> has argued that Web 3.0 is where "the computer is generating new information", rather than humans.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Others simply state their belief that Web 3.0 will primarily focus on dramatically improving the functionality and usability of search engines.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="toccolours" style="float:none; padding: 10px 15px 10px 15px; display:table;">
<p>People keep asking what Web 3.0 is. I think maybe when you've got an overlay of <a href="/wiki/Scalable_vector_graphics" title="Scalable vector graphics" class="mw-redirect">scalable vector graphics</a> – everything rippling and folding and looking misty&#160;— on <a href="/wiki/Web_2.0" title="Web 2.0">Web 2.0</a> and access to a semantic Web integrated across a huge space of data, you'll have access to an unbelievable data resource..."</p>
<p style="text-align: right;">— <cite><a href="/wiki/Tim_Berners-Lee" title="Tim Berners-Lee">Tim Berners-Lee</a>, 2006</cite></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Semantic_Web&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Need">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Need">Need</span></h3>
<p>The idea of a <i>semantic web</i>, able to describe and associate meaning with data necessarily involves more than simple XHTML mark-up code. It is based on an assumption that in order for it to be possible for machines to accurately interpret web content, far more than the mere (natural language) ordered relationships involving letters and words is necessary - as underlying infrastructure attendant to semantic issues. Otherwise, most of the supportive functionality would have been available in Web 2.0 (and before) and it would have been possible to derive a semantically capable Web with minor, incremental additions.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from April 2011">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Additions to the infrastructure to support semantic functionality include latent dynamic network models that can, under certain conditions, be 'trained' to appropriately 'learn' meaning based on order data, in the process 'learning' relationships with order (a kind of rudimentary working grammar). See for example <a href="/wiki/Latent_semantic_analysis" title="Latent semantic analysis">latent semantic analysis</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Semantic_Web&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Challenges">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Challenges">Challenges</span></h3>
<p>Some of the challenges for the Semantic Web include vastness, vagueness, uncertainty, inconsistency, and deceit. Automated reasoning systems will have to deal with all of these issues in order to deliver on the promise of the Semantic Web.</p>
<ul>
<li>Vastness: The World Wide Web contains at least <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.worldwidewebsize.com/">24 billion pages</a> as of this writing (June 13, 2010). The <a href="/wiki/SNOMED_CT" title="SNOMED CT">SNOMED CT</a> medical terminology ontology contains 370,000 class names, and existing technology has not yet been able to eliminate all semantically duplicated terms. Any automated reasoning system will have to deal with truly huge inputs.</li>
<li>Vagueness: These are imprecise concepts like "young" or "tall". This arises from the vagueness of user queries, of concepts represented by content providers, of matching query terms to provider terms and of trying to combine different knowledge bases with overlapping but subtly different concepts. <a href="/wiki/Fuzzy_logic" title="Fuzzy logic">Fuzzy logic</a> is the most common technique for dealing with vagueness.</li>
<li>Uncertainty: These are precise concepts with uncertain values. For example, a patient might present a set of symptoms which correspond to a number of different distinct diagnoses each with a different probability. <a href="/wiki/Probabilistic_logic" title="Probabilistic logic">Probabilistic</a> reasoning techniques are generally employed to address uncertainty.</li>
<li>Inconsistency: These are logical contradictions which will inevitably arise during the development of large ontologies, and when ontologies from separate sources are combined. <a href="/wiki/Deductive_reasoning" title="Deductive reasoning">Deductive reasoning</a> fails catastrophically when faced with inconsistency, because <a href="/wiki/Principle_of_explosion" title="Principle of explosion">"anything follows from a contradiction"</a>. <a href="/wiki/Defeasible_reasoning" title="Defeasible reasoning">Defeasible reasoning</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paraconsistent_logic" title="Paraconsistent logic">paraconsistent reasoning</a> are two techniques which can be employed to deal with inconsistency.</li>
<li>Deceit: This is when the producer of the information is intentionally misleading the consumer of the information. <a href="/wiki/Cryptography" title="Cryptography">Cryptography</a> techniques are currently utilized to alleviate this threat.</li>
</ul>
<p>This list of challenges is illustrative rather than exhaustive, and it focuses on the challenges to the "unifying logic" and "proof" layers of the Semantic Web. The <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web_Consortium" title="World Wide Web Consortium">World Wide Web Consortium</a> (W3C) Incubator Group for Uncertainty Reasoning for the World Wide Web (URW3-XG) <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.w3.org/2005/Incubator/urw3/XGR-urw3-20080331/">final report</a> lumps these problems together under the single heading of "uncertainty". Many of the techniques mentioned here will require extensions to the <a href="/wiki/Web_Ontology_Language" title="Web Ontology Language">Web Ontology Language</a> (OWL) for example to annotate conditional probabilities. This is an area of active research.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Semantic_Web&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Relationship to the hypertext web">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_to_the_hypertext_web">Relationship to the hypertext web</span></h2>
<p>Standardization for "Semantic Web" in the context of Web 3.0 is under the care of W3C - the very same standardization body ruling the basic standards for Web 1.0 and Web 2.0. Existing standards are referred on the respective web pages of W3C<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> as for <a href="/wiki/Resource_Description_Framework" title="Resource Description Framework">RDF</a>, <a href="/wiki/Web_Ontology_Language" title="Web Ontology Language">OWL</a>, <a href="/wiki/SPARQL" title="SPARQL">SPARQL</a>, <a href="/wiki/RDFa" title="RDFa">RDFa</a>, <a href="/wiki/SKOS" title="SKOS" class="mw-redirect">SKOS</a>, <a href="/wiki/RDFS" title="RDFS" class="mw-redirect">RDFS</a>, <a href="/wiki/GRDDL" title="GRDDL">GRDDL</a>, <a href="/wiki/POWDER" title="POWDER" class="mw-redirect">POWDER</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rule_Interchange_Format" title="Rule Interchange Format">RIF</a>, <a href="/wiki/SAWSDL" title="SAWSDL">SAWSDL</a> and many other W3C standards referred in this context.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Semantic_Web&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Current state of standardization">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Current_state_of_standardization">Current state of standardization</span></h3>
<p>The term "Semantic Web" is often used more specifically to refer to the formats and technologies that enable it<sup id="cite_ref-W3C.2C_SemWeb_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W3C.2C_SemWeb-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup>. Especially the collection, structuring and recovery of collected linked data are empowered by technologies such as RDF, SPARQL, OWL, and SKOS. These technologies are specified as respective standards of W3C and include e.g.</p>
<ul>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Resource_Description_Framework" title="Resource Description Framework">Resource Description Framework</a> (RDF) as a variety of data interchange formats</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Resource_Description_Framework" title="Resource Description Framework">RDF/XML</a> as a key example</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Notation_3" title="Notation 3" class="mw-redirect">N3</a>,</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Turtle_(syntax)" title="Turtle (syntax)">Turtle syntax</a>,</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/N-Triples" title="N-Triples">N-Triples</a>,</li>
</ul>
<p>and notations such as</p>
<ul>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/RDF_Schema" title="RDF Schema">RDF Schema</a> (RDFS)</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Web_Ontology_Language" title="Web Ontology Language">Web Ontology Language</a> (OWL),</li>
</ul>
<p>all of which are intended to provide a <a href="/wiki/Description_logic" title="Description logic">formal description</a> of <a href="/wiki/Concept" title="Concept">concepts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Terminology" title="Terminology">terms</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Causality" title="Causality">relationships</a> within a given <a href="/wiki/Knowledge_domain" title="Knowledge domain" class="mw-redirect">knowledge domain</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Semantic_Web&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Components">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Components">Components</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:396px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Semantic-web-stack.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/3/37/Semantic-web-stack.png" width="394" height="430" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">The <a href="/wiki/Semantic_Web_Stack" title="Semantic Web Stack">Semantic Web Stack</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The semantic web comprises the standards and tools of <a href="/wiki/XML" title="XML">XML</a>, <a href="/wiki/W3C_XML_Schema" title="W3C XML Schema" class="mw-redirect">XML Schema</a>, <a href="/wiki/Resource_Description_Framework" title="Resource Description Framework">RDF</a>, <a href="/wiki/RDF_Schema" title="RDF Schema">RDF Schema</a> and <a href="/wiki/Web_Ontology_Language" title="Web Ontology Language">OWL</a> that are organized in the <a href="/wiki/Semantic_Web_Stack" title="Semantic Web Stack">Semantic Web Stack</a>. The <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.w3.org/TR/owl-features/">OWL Web Ontology Language Overview</a> describes the function and relationship of each of these components of the semantic web:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/XML" title="XML">XML</a> provides an elemental syntax for content structure within documents, yet associates no semantics with the meaning of the content contained within. XML is not at present a necessary component of Semantic Web technologies in most cases, as alternative syntaxes exists, such as <a href="/wiki/Turtle_(syntax)" title="Turtle (syntax)">Turtle</a>. Turtle is a de facto standard, but has not been through a formal standardization process.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/W3C_XML_Schema" title="W3C XML Schema" class="mw-redirect">XML Schema</a> is a language for providing and restricting the structure and content of elements contained within XML documents.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Resource_Description_Framework" title="Resource Description Framework">RDF</a> is a simple language for expressing <a href="/wiki/Data_model" title="Data model">data models</a>, which refer to objects ("<a href="/wiki/Resource_(Web)" title="Resource (Web)">resources</a>") and their relationships. An RDF-based model can be represented in a variety of syntaxes, e.g., RDF/XML, N3, Turtle, and RDFa.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> RDF is a fundamental standard of the Semantic Web.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/RDF_Schema" title="RDF Schema">RDF Schema</a> extends RDF and is a vocabulary for describing properties and classes of RDF-based resources, with semantics for generalized-hierarchies of such properties and classes.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Web_Ontology_Language" title="Web Ontology Language">OWL</a> adds more vocabulary for describing properties and classes: among others, relations between classes (e.g. disjointness), cardinality (e.g. "exactly one"), equality, richer typing of properties, characteristics of properties (e.g. symmetry), and enumerated classes.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/SPARQL" title="SPARQL">SPARQL</a> is a protocol and query language for semantic web data sources.</li>
</ul>
<p>Current ongoing standardizations include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rule_Interchange_Format" title="Rule Interchange Format">Rule Interchange Format</a> (RIF) as the Rule Layer of the <a href="/wiki/Semantic_Web_Stack" title="Semantic Web Stack">Semantic Web Stack</a></li>
</ul>
<p>Not yet fully realized layers include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Unifying Logic and Proof layers are undergoing active research.</li>
</ul>
<p>The intent is to enhance the <a href="/wiki/Usability" title="Usability">usability</a> and usefulness of the Web and its interconnected <a href="/wiki/Resource_(computer_science)" title="Resource (computer science)">resources</a> through:</p>
<ul>
<li>Servers which expose existing data systems using the RDF and SPARQL standards. Many <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://esw.w3.org/topic/ConverterToRdf">converters to RDF</a> exist from different applications. <a href="/wiki/Relational_database" title="Relational database">Relational databases</a> are an important source. The semantic web server attaches to the existing system without affecting its operation.</li>
<li>Documents "marked up" with semantic information (an <a href="/wiki/Extension_(computing)" title="Extension (computing)" class="mw-redirect">extension</a> of the HTML <tt>&lt;meta&gt;</tt> <a href="/wiki/HTML_element" title="HTML element">tags</a> used in today's Web pages to supply <a href="/wiki/Information" title="Information">information</a> for <a href="/wiki/Web_search_engine" title="Web search engine">Web search engines</a> using <a href="/wiki/Web_crawler" title="Web crawler">web crawlers</a>). This could be <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">machine-understandable</a> information about the human-understandable content of the document (such as the creator, title, description, etc., of the document) or it could be purely <a href="/wiki/Metadata_(computing)" title="Metadata (computing)" class="mw-redirect">metadata</a> representing a set of facts (such as resources and services elsewhere in the site). (Note that <i>anything</i> that can be identified with a <i>Uniform Resource Identifier</i> (<a href="/wiki/Uniform_Resource_Identifier" title="Uniform Resource Identifier">URI</a>) can be described, so the semantic web can reason about animals, people, places, ideas, etc.) Semantic markup is often generated automatically, rather than manually.</li>
<li>Common metadata vocabularies (<a href="/wiki/Ontology_(information_science)" title="Ontology (information science)">ontologies</a>) and maps between vocabularies that allow document creators to know how to mark up their documents so that agents can use the information in the supplied metadata (so that <i>Author</i> in the sense of 'the Author of the page' won't be confused with <i>Author</i> in the sense of a book that is the subject of a book review).</li>
<li>Automated agents to perform tasks for users of the semantic web using this data</li>
<li>Web-based services (often with agents of their own) to supply information specifically to agents (for example, a <a href="/w/index.php?title=Trust_service&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Trust service (page does not exist)">Trust service</a> that an agent could ask if some online store has a history of poor service or <a href="/wiki/Spamming" title="Spamming" class="mw-redirect">spamming</a>)</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Semantic_Web&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Limitations of HTML">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Limitations_of_HTML">Limitations of HTML</span></h3>
<p>Many files on a typical computer can be loosely divided into human readable <a href="/wiki/Document" title="Document">documents</a> and machine readable <a href="/wiki/Data" title="Data">data</a>. Documents like mail messages, reports, and brochures are read by humans. Data, like calendars, addressbooks, playlists, and spreadsheets are presented using an application program which lets them be viewed, searched and combined in different ways.</p>
<p>Currently, the World Wide Web is based mainly on documents written in <a href="/wiki/Hypertext_Markup_Language" title="Hypertext Markup Language" class="mw-redirect">Hypertext Markup Language</a> (<a href="/wiki/HTML" title="HTML">HTML</a>), a markup convention that is used for coding a body of text interspersed with multimedia objects such as images and interactive forms. Metadata tags, for example</p>
<div dir="ltr" class="mw-geshi" style="text-align: left;">
<div class="html4strict source-html4strict">
<pre class="de1">
<span class="sc2">&lt;<span class="kw2">meta</span> <span class="kw3">name</span><span class="sy0">=</span><span class="st0">"keywords"</span> <span class="kw3">content</span><span class="sy0">=</span><span class="st0">"computing, computer studies, computer"</span>&gt;</span>
<span class="sc2">&lt;<span class="kw2">meta</span> <span class="kw3">name</span><span class="sy0">=</span><span class="st0">"description"</span> <span class="kw3">content</span><span class="sy0">=</span><span class="st0">"Cheap widgets for sale"</span>&gt;</span>
<span class="sc2">&lt;<span class="kw2">meta</span> <span class="kw3">name</span><span class="sy0">=</span><span class="st0">"author"</span> <span class="kw3">content</span><span class="sy0">=</span><span class="st0">"John Doe"</span>&gt;</span>
</pre></div>
</div>
<p>provide a method by which computers can categorise the content of web pages.</p>
<p>With HTML and a tool to render it (perhaps <a href="/wiki/Web_browser" title="Web browser">web browser</a> software, perhaps another <a href="/wiki/User_agent" title="User agent">user agent</a>), one can create and present a page that lists items for sale. The HTML of this catalog page can make simple, document-level assertions such as "this document's title is 'Widget Superstore'", but there is no capability within the HTML itself to assert unambiguously that, for example, item number X586172 is an Acme Gizmo with a retail price of €199, or that it is a consumer product. Rather, HTML can only say that the span of text "X586172" is something that should be positioned near "Acme Gizmo" and "€199", etc. There is no way to say "this is a catalog" or even to establish that "Acme Gizmo" is a kind of title or that "€199" is a price. There is also no way to express that these pieces of information are bound together in describing a discrete item, distinct from other items perhaps listed on the page.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Semantic_HTML" title="Semantic HTML">Semantic HTML</a> refers to the traditional HTML practice of markup following intention, rather than specifying layout details directly. For example, the use of <code>&lt;em&gt;</code> denoting "emphasis" rather than <code>&lt;i&gt;</code>, which specifies <a href="/wiki/Italics" title="Italics" class="mw-redirect">italics</a>. Layout details are left up to the browser, in combination with <a href="/wiki/Cascading_Style_Sheets" title="Cascading Style Sheets">Cascading Style Sheets</a>. But this practice falls short of specifying the semantics of objects such as items for sale or prices.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Microformat" title="Microformat">Microformats</a> represent unofficial attempts to extend HTML syntax to create <a href="/wiki/Machine-readable" title="Machine-readable" class="mw-redirect">machine-readable</a> semantic markup about objects such as retail stores and items for sale.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Semantic_Web&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Semantic Web solutions">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Semantic_Web_solutions">Semantic Web solutions</span></h3>
<p>The Semantic Web takes the solution further. It involves publishing in languages specifically designed for data: <a href="/wiki/Resource_Description_Framework" title="Resource Description Framework">Resource Description Framework</a> (RDF), <a href="/wiki/Web_Ontology_Language" title="Web Ontology Language">Web Ontology Language</a> (OWL), and Extensible Markup Language (<a href="/wiki/XML" title="XML">XML</a>). HTML describes documents and the links between them. RDF, OWL, and XML, by contrast, can describe arbitrary things such as people, meetings, or airplane parts.</p>
<p>These technologies are combined in order to provide descriptions that supplement or replace the content of Web documents. Thus, content may manifest itself as descriptive data stored in Web-accessible <a href="/wiki/Database" title="Database">databases</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> or as markup within documents (particularly, in Extensible HTML (<a href="/wiki/XHTML" title="XHTML">XHTML</a>) interspersed with XML, or, more often, purely in XML, with layout or rendering cues stored separately). The machine-readable descriptions enable content managers to add meaning to the content, i.e., to describe the structure of the knowledge we have about that content. In this way, a machine can process knowledge itself, instead of text, using processes similar to human <a href="/wiki/Deductive_reasoning" title="Deductive reasoning">deductive reasoning</a> and <a href="/wiki/Inference" title="Inference">inference</a>, thereby obtaining more meaningful results and helping computers to perform automated information gathering and <a href="/wiki/Research" title="Research">research</a>.</p>
<p>An example of a tag that would be used in a non-semantic web page:</p>
<div dir="ltr" class="mw-geshi" style="text-align: left;">
<div class="xml source-xml">
<pre class="de1">
<span class="sc3"><span class="re1">&lt;item<span class="re2">&gt;</span></span></span>cat<span class="sc3"><span class="re1">&lt;/item<span class="re2">&gt;</span></span></span>
</pre></div>
</div>
<p>Encoding similar information in a semantic web page might look like this:</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Tim_Berners-Lee" title="Tim Berners-Lee">Tim Berners-Lee</a> calls the resulting network of <a href="/wiki/Linked_Data" title="Linked Data">Linked Data</a> the <a href="/wiki/Giant_Global_Graph" title="Giant Global Graph">Giant Global Graph</a>, in contrast to the HTML-based <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a>. Berners-Lee posits that if past was document sharing, the future is data sharing. His answer to the question of "how" provides three points of instruction. One, a URL should point to the data. Two, anyone accessing the URL should get data back. Three, relationships in the data should point to additional URLs with data.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Semantic_Web&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Skeptical reactions">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Skeptical_reactions">Skeptical reactions</span></h2>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Semantic_Web&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Practical feasibility">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Practical_feasibility">Practical feasibility</span></h3>
<p>Critics (e.g. <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://portal.acm.org/citation.cfm?id=900051.900063&amp;coll=ACM&amp;dl=ACM&amp;CFID=29933182&amp;CFTOKEN=24611642">Which Semantic Web?</a>) question the basic feasibility of a complete or even partial fulfillment of the semantic web. <a href="/wiki/Cory_Doctorow" title="Cory Doctorow">Cory Doctorow</a>'s critique ("<a href="/wiki/Metacrap" title="Metacrap">metacrap</a>") is from the perspective of human behavior and personal preferences. For example, people lie: they may include spurious metadata into Web pages in an attempt to mislead Semantic Web engines that naively assume the metadata's veracity. This phenomenon was well-known with metatags that fooled the AltaVista ranking algorithm into elevating the ranking of certain Web pages: the Google indexing engine specifically looks for such attempts at manipulation. <a href="/wiki/Peter_G%C3%A4rdenfors" title="Peter Gärdenfors">Peter Gärdenfors</a> and <a href="/wiki/Timo_Honkela" title="Timo Honkela">Timo Honkela</a> point out that logic-based semantic web technologies cover only a fraction of the relevant phenomena related to semantics.<sup id="cite_ref-Gardenfors04_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gardenfors04-26"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Honkela08_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Honkela08-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Where semantic web technologies have found a greater degree of practical adoption, it has tended to be among core specialized communities and organizations for intra-company projects.<sup id="cite_ref-Herman000_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Herman000-28"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> The practical constraints toward adoption have appeared less challenging where domain and scope is more limited than that of the general public and the World-Wide Web.<sup id="cite_ref-Herman000_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Herman000-28"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Semantic_Web&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Potential of an idea in fast progress">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Potential_of_an_idea_in_fast_progress">Potential of an idea in fast progress</span></h3>
<p>The original 2001 <a href="/wiki/Scientific_American" title="Scientific American">Scientific American</a> article by Berners-Lee described an expected evolution of the existing Web to a Semantic Web.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> A complete evolution as described by Berners-Lee has yet to occur. In 2006, Berners-Lee and colleagues stated that: "This simple idea, however, remains largely unrealized."<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> While the idea is still in the making, it seems to evolve quickly and inspire many.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from July 2011">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Between 2007–2010 several scholars have explored first applications and the social potential of the semantic web in the business and health sectors, and for social networking<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> and for the broader evolution of democracy, specifically, how a society forms its common will in a democratic manner through a semantic web<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Semantic_Web&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Censorship and privacy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Censorship_and_privacy">Censorship and privacy</span></h3>
<p>Enthusiasm about the semantic web could be tempered by concerns regarding <a href="/wiki/Internet_censorship" title="Internet censorship">censorship</a> and <a href="/wiki/Privacy" title="Privacy">privacy</a>. For instance, <a href="/wiki/Intelligent_text_analysis" title="Intelligent text analysis" class="mw-redirect">text-analyzing</a> techniques can now be easily bypassed by using other words, metaphors for instance, or by using images in place of words. An advanced implementation of the semantic web would make it much easier for governments to control the viewing and creation of online information, as this information would be much easier for an automated content-blocking machine to understand. In addition, the issue has also been raised that, with the use of <a href="/wiki/FOAF_(software)" title="FOAF (software)">FOAF</a> files and geo location <a href="/wiki/Meta-data" title="Meta-data" class="mw-redirect">meta-data</a>, there would be very little anonymity associated with the authorship of articles on things such as a personal <a href="/wiki/Blog" title="Blog">blog</a>. Some of these concerns were addressed in the "Policy Aware Web" project<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> and is an active research and development topic.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Semantic_Web&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Doubling output formats">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Doubling_output_formats">Doubling output formats</span></h3>
<p>Another criticism of the semantic web is that it would be much more time-consuming to create and publish content because there would need to be two formats for one piece of data: one for human viewing and one for machines. However, many <a href="/wiki/Web_application" title="Web application">web applications</a> in development are addressing this issue by creating a machine-readable format upon the publishing of data or the request of a machine for such data. The development of <a href="/wiki/Microformat" title="Microformat">microformats</a> has been one reaction to this kind of criticism. Another argument in defense of the feasibility of semantic web is the likely falling price of human intelligence tasks in digital labor markets, such as the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_Mechanical_Turk" title="Amazon Mechanical Turk">Amazon Mechanical Turk</a>.</p>
<p>Specifications such as <a href="/wiki/ERDF_(data_format)" title="ERDF (data format)" class="mw-redirect">eRDF</a> and <a href="/wiki/RDFa" title="RDFa">RDFa</a> allow arbitrary <a href="/wiki/Resource_Description_Framework" title="Resource Description Framework">RDF</a> data to be embedded in HTML pages. The <a href="/wiki/GRDDL" title="GRDDL">GRDDL</a> (Gleaning Resource Descriptions from Dialects of Language) mechanism allows existing material (including microformats) to be automatically interpreted as <a href="/wiki/Resource_Description_Framework" title="Resource Description Framework">RDF</a>, so publishers only need to use a single format, such as HTML.</p>

<p>This section lists some of the many projects and tools that exist to create Semantic Web solutions.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Semantic_Web&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: DBpedia">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="DBpedia">DBpedia</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/DBpedia" title="DBpedia">DBpedia</a> is an effort to publish structured data extracted from Wikipedia: the data is published in RDF and made available on the Web for use under the <a href="/wiki/GNU_Free_Documentation_License" title="GNU Free Documentation License">GNU Free Documentation License</a>, thus allowing Semantic Web agents to provide inferencing and advanced querying over the Wikipedia-derived dataset and facilitating interlinking, re-use and extension in other data-sources.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Semantic_Web&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: FOAF">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="FOAF">FOAF</span></h3>
<p>A popular application of the semantic web is <a href="/wiki/FOAF_(software)" title="FOAF (software)">Friend of a Friend</a> (or FoaF), which uses RDF to describe the relationships people have to other people and the "things" around them. FOAF permits intelligent agents to make sense of the thousands of connections people have with each other, their jobs and the items important to their lives; connections that may or may not be enumerated in searches using traditional web search engines. Because the connections are so vast in number, human interpretation of the information may not be the best way of analyzing them.</p>
<p>FOAF is an example of how the Semantic Web attempts to make use of the relationships within a social context.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Semantic_Web&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: GoodRelations for e-commerce">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="GoodRelations_for_e-commerce">GoodRelations for e-commerce</span></h3>
<p>A huge potential for Semantic Web technologies lies in adding data structure and typed links to the vast amount of offer data, product model features, and tendering / request for quotation data.</p>
<p>The GoodRelations ontology<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> is a popular vocabulary for expressing product information, prices, payment options, etc. It also allows expressing demand in a straightforward fashion.</p>
<p>GoodRelations has been adopted by Google, BestBuy, Overstock, Yahoo, OpenLink Software, O'Reilly Media, the Book Mashup, and many others.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Semantic_Web&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: SIOC">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="SIOC">SIOC</span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Semantically-Interlinked_Online_Communities" title="Semantically-Interlinked Online Communities">Semantically-Interlinked Online Communities</a> project (SIOC, pronounced "shock") provides a vocabulary of terms and relationships that model web data spaces. Examples of such data spaces include, among others: discussion forums, <a href="/wiki/Blog" title="Blog">blogs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blogroll" title="Blogroll" class="mw-redirect">blogrolls</a> / feed subscriptions, <a href="/wiki/Mailing_list" title="Mailing list">mailing lists</a>, shared bookmarks and image galleries.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Semantic_Web&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: SIMILE">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="SIMILE">SIMILE</span></h3>
<p><b>S</b>emantic <b>I</b>nteroperability of <b>M</b>etadata and <b>I</b>nformation in un<b>L</b>ike <b>E</b>nvironments</p>
<p><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://simile.mit.edu/">SIMILE</a> was a joint project, conducted by the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://libraries.mit.edu/">MIT Libraries</a> and <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://csail.mit.edu/">MIT CSAIL</a> and funded by the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://secure.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/wiki/Andrew_W._Mellon_Foundation">Mellon Foundation</a>, which sought to enhance interoperability among digital assets, schemata/vocabularies/ontologies, meta data, and services. With completion of the project, many of its tools were open sourced and spun out to <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://simile-widgets.org">simile-widgets</a>, a community-managed site.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Semantic_Web&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: NextBio">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="NextBio">NextBio</span></h3>
<p>A database consolidating high-throughput life sciences experimental data tagged and connected via biomedical ontologies. <a href="/wiki/Nextbio" title="Nextbio">Nextbio</a> is accessible via a search engine interface. Researchers can contribute their findings for incorporation to the database. The database currently supports gene or protein expression data and sequence centric data and is steadily expanding to support other biological data types.</p>

<p><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.mkbergman.com/wp-content/themes/ai3/images/2008Posts/080922_lod-datasets.png">Datasets in the Linking Open Data project, as of Sept 2008</a></p>

<p>The <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://esw.w3.org/topic/SweoIG/TaskForces/CommunityProjects/LinkingOpenData">Linking Open Data project</a> is a W3C-led effort to create openly accessible, and interlinked, RDF Data on the Web. The data in question takes the form of <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://esw.w3.org/topic/TaskForces/CommunityProjects/LinkingOpenData/DataSets">RDF Data Sets</a> drawn from a broad collection of data sources. There is a focus on the <a href="/wiki/Linked_Data" title="Linked Data">Linked Data</a> style of publishing RDF on the Web.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Semantic_Web&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: OpenPSI">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="OpenPSI">OpenPSI</span></h3>
<p>OpenPSI the (<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.openpsi.org">OpenPSI project</a>) is a community effort to create a UK government linked data service that supports research. It is a collaboration between the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Southampton" title="University of Southampton">University of Southampton</a> and the UK government, led by <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Public_Sector_Information" title="Office of Public Sector Information">OPSI</a> at <a href="/wiki/The_National_Archives" title="The National Archives" class="mw-redirect">The National Archives</a> and is supported by <a href="/wiki/JISC" title="JISC" class="mw-redirect">JISC</a> funding.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Semantic_Web&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: GoPubMed">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="GoPubMed">GoPubMed</span></h3>
<p>Is the first semantic search engine for the life sciences. It uses the GeneOntology (GO) and the Medical Subject Headings (MeSH) to semantically filter millions of biomedical abstracts from MEDLINE. The public website <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.GoPubMed.com">GoPubMed.com</a> allows users to verify papers including their background information.</p>
